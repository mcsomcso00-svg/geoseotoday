# GEO Tools for 2026: The 8 Worth Using

**You don't need a stack of subscriptions to do GEO. In 2026 there are really only four jobs worth tooling for: AI citation monitoring, content-readiness scoring, schema / structured data, and crawler-access checks.** This piece covers 8 tools across those four jobs — half of them free — and tells you exactly when it's worth paying for the rest.

## First, get clear on what you want a tool to solve

The full GEO loop is "write citable content → make sure AI crawlers can read it → track whether you get cited." Tools only speed up those three steps; they can't replace the content itself. There are dozens of SaaS products flying the GEO flag in 2026, priced anywhere from $20 to $500 a month, and most of them overlap heavily. The smart buying order is simple: **run the whole loop on free tools first, find out where your bottleneck actually is, then pay to fix that one bottleneck.**

## Job 1: Content-readiness scoring (use before you publish)

1. **GEO Readiness Checker (GeoSeoToday, free)** — paste your draft into the [GEO Readiness Checker](/en/geo/) and get a 0–100 score in 30 seconds, with a line-by-line pass over 9 signals: answer-first paragraph, data density, FAQ, structural depth, and more. Perfect as the last gate before you hit publish.
2. **General-purpose LLMs (ChatGPT / Claude, free or ~$20/mo)** — not for writing the article, but for *simulating a citation*: hand the model your target question and watch how it would summarize your page, and what's missing that stops it from quoting you. It's the cheapest GEO test there is.

## Job 2: AI citation monitoring (use after you publish)

3. **AI-visibility platforms like Profound and Otterly.ai (paid)** — track, in bulk, how often your brand shows up (and gets cited) in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and AI Overview answers. Worth it for a team managing 50+ target questions at once; a small site can get by with manual checks.
4. **A manual query sheet (free)** — list 10–20 core questions in a spreadsheet and, once a month, run them past the three big engines and log which sources got cited. Low-tech but effective — and it's exactly what the paid platforms do under the hood.

## Job 3: Schema and structured data

5. **Schema Markup Generator (free generators like Merkle's)** — spin up Article, FAQPage, and HowTo JSON-LD fast, ready to copy and paste.
6. **Google Rich Results Test (free)** — validate that your schema is valid. One caveat: it checks eligibility for Google's rich results specifically, but the same JSON-LD is an entity signal to other AI engines just the same.

## Job 4: Crawler-access checks

7. **robots.txt check + server logs (free)** — confirm GPTBot, PerplexityBot, ClaudeBot, and Google-Extended aren't being blocked. Plenty of sites never get GEO off the ground for one reason only: back in 2023 some engineer set every AI crawler to Disallow.
8. **Screaming Frog (free version crawls 500 URLs)** — use a custom User-Agent to mimic an AI crawler and check whether your important pages need JavaScript to render their content. Most AI crawlers don't execute JavaScript — and that's fatal.

## FAQ

**Q1: Is there one tool that does all of GEO?**
No. GEO spans content quality, technical access, and results monitoring, and as of 2026 no single tool does all three well — the ones claiming to cover everything usually do each piece shallowly.

**Q2: Are paid AI-monitoring platforms worth buying?**
Ask yourself how many questions you're tracking first. Under 20, manual checks take less than two hours a month; once you're past 50, or need multilingual tracking across markets, a paid platform starts to pay for itself.

**Q3: Do I still need traditional SEO tools (Ahrefs, Semrush)?**
Yes. GEO is built on top of SEO — keyword research and backlink analysis still run on the traditional tools. The two stack; they don't replace each other.

**Q4: Do AI writing tools count as GEO tools?**
Not at the core. AI can speed up drafting, but what decides whether you get cited is structure and specificity, and a writing tool can't solve that for you.
