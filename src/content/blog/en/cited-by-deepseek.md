# How to Get Cited by DeepSeek — Sourcing Differences in Chinese-Language AI

**To get cited by DeepSeek, the move is not translating your English into Chinese — it's providing sources that are natively written in Chinese and factually complete. DeepSeek mostly answers native Chinese speakers, so it favors natural-sounding, fact-dense Chinese content, and tends to demote machine-translated versions as low quality.** For sites that maintain genuine Chinese content, that's a lower-competition citation opportunity Western AI barely covers.

## How does DeepSeek's sourcing differ from ChatGPT's?

DeepSeek is a model trained by a Chinese team on a Chinese-heavy corpus, and it leans on the Chinese-language web when it retrieves. That produces three sourcing preferences clearly different from Western AI: **language nativeness, fact density, and source structure.**

Most "get cited by AI" playbooks write English the ChatGPT way and machine-translate it into Chinese. That barely works on DeepSeek — its daily reality is "asked in Chinese, answer in Chinese," and a passage with heavy translation-ese and unnatural word order reads as a weak-quality source to it. The table below puts the two engine types side by side, so you can see why the same content performs so differently:

| Sourcing dimension | Western AI (ChatGPT / Perplexity) | DeepSeek (Chinese context) |
|---|---|---|
| Language preference | Follows query language; English corpus deepest | Strong preference for native, natural-sounding Chinese |
| Machine-translated text | Acceptable for English queries | Translated zh (Trad/Simp) often demoted as low quality |
| Fact density | Values self-contained answer blocks | Same, but weighs completeness of data and named terms even more |
| Source structure | Schema, clear entities | Clear subheads and definition sentences, plus Chinese-site entity consistency |
| Freshness | Prefers recently updated | Prefers recent, with more immediate Chinese-current-events coverage |

The takeaway is blunt: the ticket to a DeepSeek citation is native Chinese content quality, not merely "having a Chinese version."

## Why does native Chinese actually have an edge?

Because most international sites' Chinese content is machine-translated or thinly localized, "high-quality native Chinese sources" are genuinely scarce when DeepSeek faces a Chinese query. Just by writing naturally and completely, you're competing in an under-supplied pool.

Three concrete advantages:

1. **Low competition density** — most English sites won't seriously rewrite content for Chinese, so truly native, in-depth Chinese articles are far fewer than English ones.
2. **Nativeness is itself a quality signal** — DeepSeek is sensitive to whether Chinese word order and phrasing are idiomatic; native writing naturally beats translation-ese.
3. **Local facts fit better** — Chinese readers often ask about local situations, and local data, cases, and regulatory detail are exactly what machine translation can't supply.

The flip side to watch: mixing up Traditional and Simplified backfires. Converting Simplified content straight to Traditional (or vice versa) without changing the vocabulary is something DeepSeek can tell is "transcoding," not localization — don't expect a character swap to fool it.

## 7 steps to get Chinese content into DeepSeek's citation pool

This checklist turns the sourcing preferences above into executable actions. Work through it and your Chinese page will meet the baseline for a DeepSeek citation:

1. **Write natively, don't machine-translate** — conceive and write directly in Chinese rather than translating from English; at minimum have a native speaker fix word order and phrasing.
2. **Answer-first** — open each H2 with a 40–80 character self-contained conclusion the model can lift whole.
3. **Raise fact density** — spell out concrete numbers, dates, tool names, and spec limits; avoid empty phrases like "works great."
4. **Make Traditional and Simplified each native** — rebuild the vocabulary for each (e.g. 軟體/软件, 行動/移动, 程式/程序); don't character-convert between them.
5. **Clear Chinese subheads and definition sentences** — use the questions readers actually ask as H2s, and lead paragraphs with a quotable definition sentence.
6. **Consistent Chinese entity** — keep byline, brand name, and About page consistent site-wide so the model treats you as a defined entity, not a generic mention.
7. **Confirm you're crawlable** — don't blanket-block non-Western crawlers in robots.txt or your CDN, and keep important content in the HTML source rather than JS-rendered only.

## How to verify whether DeepSeek is citing you

The method is as direct as for any AI: take the target questions your article aims at, ask DeepSeek in Chinese, and log whether its answer mentions or links you and which sources it cites. Build a tracking sheet — test 10–20 questions monthly, and run the same batch through ChatGPT and Perplexity for comparison. You'll quickly see which content only DeepSeek cites versus only Western AI, and close the gaps accordingly. Citation changes usually show up within 4–8 weeks of a content rewrite.

## FAQ

**Q1: If I machine-translate an English article into Chinese, will DeepSeek cite it?**
Unlikely. Raw machine translation without editing has unnatural word order and phrasing, and DeepSeek tends to rate it a low-quality source. At minimum have a native speaker rewrite the phrasing — ideally write natively in Chinese.

**Q2: If I only publish Simplified, will DeepSeek cite it for Traditional queries?**
It might, but it's not ideal. DeepSeek reads Simplified fine, yet for a Traditional query it prefers native Traditional sources; if you serve Taiwan/HK markets, ship a native Traditional version rather than just Simplified.

**Q3: Does DeepSeek only look at websites inside China?**
No, but the Chinese-language web carries more weight. An overseas Chinese-language site can still enter the citation pool as long as its content is native, complete, and crawlable — quality matters more than where it's registered.

**Q4: For the same article, is writing for a DeepSeek citation the same as for ChatGPT?**
The skeleton is the same (answer-first, fact density, clear structure); the difference is language nativeness. DeepSeek demands more from Chinese phrasing, and its penalty for machine translation is more pronounced than ChatGPT's.
