# Comparison / vs Pages: How to Write Them Without Getting Flagged as Spam

**The key to a comparison page that never gets flagged as spam is to write it like a review page, not a marketing page.** That means four things — show first-hand evidence of use (screenshots, real test data), quantify the differences, be willing to write down where your own product loses, and label your prices with a check date. Self-congratulatory comparison pages churned out from a template are the textbook form of low-end programmatic SEO, and they get caught sooner or later.

## Why are comparison pages such a disaster zone?

Because "A vs B" and "A alternative" are the most valuable terms in commercial intent — the person searching has already reached the final step before buying. So swarms of SaaS companies use one template to mass-produce dozens of "us vs the competition" pages, and every page concludes with a clean sweep in their own favor. Since 2022 Google has folded its Reviews System into the core algorithm, explicitly demanding that review-style content demonstrate first-hand experience and evidence; and **in the eyes of the engine, a comparison page is a kind of review page**, so the same standard applies. When batch-produced brag pages run into that system, the mild outcome is sinking out of sight and the severe one is dragging down the quality rating of your whole site.

## The four traits that get you flagged as spam (confess first)

1. **Template mass production**: 50 comparison pages that only swap the brand name and logo, with identical table structures and identical sentence patterns — this is the low-end form of programmatic SEO, and the pattern is obvious at a glance.
2. **The clean-sweep conclusion**: every row has a green check for you and a red X for the competitor. Users don't believe it, and neither does the engine.
3. **Zero evidence of use**: not a single screenshot of you actually operating the product, not one real measured number — the whole page is rewritten from both sides' official feature lists.
4. **No timestamp**: no note on when the pricing was pulled, no last-updated date. Tool pricing changes several times a year, so stale information is itself a quality problem.

## The six requirements of a comparison page that passes

| Requirement | What it looks like in practice |
|---|---|
| First-hand experience | Actually sign up for both products and use each for 1–2 weeks; include your own screenshots |
| Quantified differences | "Exporting 10,000 records: A took 12 seconds, B took 47" — not "A is faster" |
| Willing to say you lose | At least 2–3 items where you plainly state the competitor does it better, and which kind of user should pick them |
| Price + date | State "pricing checked in September 2026" and show the page's updated date |
| Conclusions by scenario | "Small teams pick A; if you need an API, pick B" — not a single winner |
| Named author | Who ran the test and what their background is — a real byline, not "the editorial team" |

Of these, "willing to say where you lose" is the lever of trust: only when users see you admit a weakness will they believe the strengths you list. It also happens to be exactly the shape of content AI engines prefer when picking sources to cite — a passage with concrete data and a two-sided account is far more likely to get cited than a passage of one-sided self-praise.

## "Bulk" isn't the original sin — hollowness is

Programmatic SEO isn't a violation in itself: flight pages and exchange-rate pages are legitimate programmatic applications. The test is this: **does each page carry substantial content unique to that page?** You can share a page skeleton, but the real test data, screenshots, and scenario conclusions for each comparison pairing have to be genuine and unique to that pairing. If you can't manage that, make fewer pages: 5 solid comparison pages beat 50 hollow ones — and the hollow ones will drag the solid ones down with them.

## Pre-publish checklist

- How many screenshots on this page did we generate ourselves? (target ≥3)
- How many real, measured numbers are there? (target ≥5)
- How many items plainly state where we lose? (target ≥2)
- Does the pricing note the month it was checked?
- Cover the brand names — can you still tell this page apart from the competitor's comparison page? If you can't, it's a template piece.

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

**Q1: Won't writing where I lose cost me customers?**
You'll lose the customers who were never a good fit for you in the first place, and that's not a loss. What you gain in return is page credibility, higher-quality conversions, and the ranking eligibility that review-style content earns.

**Q2: Can I write a comparison page for a competitor I've never used?**
Not recommended. At minimum, sign up for the free or trial version and actually operate it before you write. If you genuinely can't get access, state plainly "this item is based on official documentation, not tested" to distinguish the level of evidence.

**Q3: How often should a comparison page be updated?**
Check prices and features at least every 6 months, update immediately on any major release, and let the page display its updated date. A stale comparison page hurts trust more than none at all.

**Q4: How many comparison pages should I make at once?**
Start with the 3–5 competitors your real market most often compares you against, and do those deeply and truthfully; expand later if you have the capacity.
