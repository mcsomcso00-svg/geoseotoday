# Link Reclamation: Turn Unlinked Brand Mentions into Backlinks

**Link reclamation means finding every page across the web that already mentions your brand, product, or founder without linking to you, then asking the owner to add the link — this kind of outreach typically gets a 30–50% reply rate, far above the 5–10% you get from cold link building, because the person already knows you and simply forgot the hyperlink.**

## Why unlinked mentions are the easiest backlinks to get

Cold outreach has to convince a stranger that your content is worth citing in the first place. Link reclamation skips that step entirely — they already wrote about you, which proves the content was worth mentioning; all that's left is a small technical fix. Most editors don't skip the link on purpose — they were rushed when writing and never looked up your URL, or pasted plain text into a CMS field and forgot to wrap it in a hyperlink tag. That's why reclamation emails can be direct: "thanks for mentioning us — here's the correct link for your readers," rather than the extended pitch cold outreach requires to justify why you deserve a link at all.

## Monitoring the web for mentions: three methods, free to paid

1. **Google Alerts (free)**: Set up alerts for your brand name, product name, and founder's name, delivered daily or weekly. Downside: noticeable delay, and it frequently misses smaller blogs.
2. **Ahrefs Content Explorer / Mentions (paid)**: Search your brand keyword and filter for results that don't link to your domain, while seeing each source's Domain Rating (DR) so you can prioritize your outreach list by authority.
3. **Manual search operator (free)**: `"your brand name" -site:yourdomain.com` — run this once a month for a small-scale sweep. Fine for smaller companies with a modest mention volume.

These three approaches aren't mutually exclusive: Google Alerts catches new mentions in real time, Ahrefs surfaces the accumulated backlog of historical mentions, and the manual search operator catches long-tail pages the first two miss.

## Outreach template: three paragraphs, readable in 60 seconds

The most common mistake in reclamation outreach is writing it like a formal memo. An effective template has exactly three parts:

1. **Point to the exact spot**: "In paragraph 3 of [article title], you mentioned our [product/service]" — include the precise passage or a screenshot to prove you actually read it, not a form letter.
2. **Make the polite ask**: "Would you mind adding a link to [exact URL] there? It'll make it easier for your readers to find us directly" — give the precise target URL so there's no lookup cost on their end.
3. **Close with zero pressure**: "Either way, thanks for mentioning us" — no push, no guilt trip. Counterintuitively, this raises reply rates rather than lowering them.

## Three types of unlinked mentions, ranked by priority

| Mention type | How to spot it | Recommended action |
|---|---|---|
| Plain-text brand/product name mention | Site search your brand name, manually check for a link | Highest priority — best reply rate |
| Cites your data or research without linking the source | Search for keywords from data you've published | Second priority — these pages usually carry more authority |
| Dead link from a redesign or URL restructure | Pull the "lost backlinks" report in Ahrefs | Ask them to update to the new URL — link reclamation plus a 404 fix in one move |

## Link reclamation vs. cold outreach: a completely different cost structure

Cold outreach has to clear two layers of uncertainty: first finding who might be willing to link to you, then convincing them why it's worth it. Stack those together and reply rates commonly sit in the single digits to around 10%. Link reclamation eliminates the first layer entirely — your monitoring tools have already told you who mentioned you — leaving only the second layer, and that layer requires almost no persuasion (you're asking for a link fix, not a change of position). That's the core reason reply rates land at 30–50%. In practice, put link reclamation at the front of your outreach schedule: for the same time investment, clear out the backlog of people who already chose to mention you before moving on to cold outreach, which requires convincing strangers from scratch. Across multiple outreach rounds the GeoSeoToday team ran in 2026, reclamation emails consistently landed reply rates 4–5x higher than cold outreach, which is why the priority ordering holds up in practice.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q1: Does link reclamation work for a brand-new site?**
It works, but only once your brand already has some visibility — a brand-new site with zero prior mentions has no backlog to reclaim. In that case, start with guest posting or digital PR to build an initial base of mentions, then circle back to link reclamation.

**Q2: What's a reasonable reply rate for a link reclamation campaign?**
30–50% is the normal range. If your rate is noticeably lower, the email is likely too generic, or you're targeting low-authority mentions such as low-quality forum posts.

**Q3: How many follow-ups should I send if there's no reply?**
One follow-up, sent 7–10 days after the first email, is enough. Since the request itself carries a low cost for the recipient, repeated follow-ups start to feel pushy and the return on the extra effort drops fast.

**Q4: Could link reclamation be seen as manipulative by search algorithms?**
No. You aren't manufacturing a new, artificial link relationship — you're completing a technical fix on a mention that already genuinely exists. That's fundamentally different from buying links or using a link farm.
