# How to Write an Answer-First Opening Paragraph (With Examples)

**An answer-first paragraph means this: your article's very first paragraph uses 40–80 words to fully answer the question in the title — self-contained, specific, and quotable on its own.** It's the highest-weighted of the nine GEO signals, because when an AI engine generates an answer, this is the paragraph it lifts most often.

## Why the first 80 words decide everything

Two reasons. For the AI: when an engine extracts a passage to answer a question, the self-contained paragraph that sits earliest and packs the highest information density has a natural edge. The 2023 paper *GEO: Generative Engine Optimization* found empirically that "clear, direct statements" are one of the most effective ways to raise visibility — up to a 40% lift. For the human: users skim between search results and AI answers, and if they don't see the answer in the first 3 seconds they leave. Answer-first rescues your bounce rate at the same time.

Traditional writing teaches you to build up and save the conclusion for the end. GEO writing does the opposite: **put the conclusion first, and make everything after it the argument and the detail.** This isn't laziness — it's journalism's inverted pyramid, revived for the AI era.

## Four rules for writing it

1. **Length: 40–80 words.** Too short can't carry a complete answer; too long and it stops being "one quotable answer." The GeoSeoToday checker treats 30–140 words as the acceptable band, with 40–80 as the sweet spot.
2. **Self-contained.** Paste this paragraph alone into a chat window and the reader should understand it without ever seeing the title. Write subjects in full — don't open with "it" or "this method."
3. **Answer the title directly.** If the title asks "how to write it," the first paragraph states the method; if the title asks "what is it," the first paragraph gives the definition.
4. **Mark it in bold.** Bold the whole paragraph or its opening sentence to flag "this is the answer" to both readers and engines.

## Six before-and-after examples

**Example 1 (definition type)**
- ❌ "In an age of information overload, more and more people are paying attention to GEO…" (warm-up, zero information)
- ✅ "GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) is the practice of optimizing content so AI engines like ChatGPT and Perplexity cite it as a source in their answers."

**Example 2 (how-to type)**
- ❌ "Want to know how it's done? Let's dive in!" (hides the answer)
- ✅ "To get cited by Perplexity, do three things: let PerplexityBot crawl your site, make every H2 section self-contained, and back claims with specific data."

**Example 3 (comparison type)**
- ❌ "Both have their pros and cons, and they suit different people." (splits the difference, zero specifics)
- ✅ "SEO chases rankings and clicks; GEO chases AI citations and brand exposure. They share the same foundation of semantic authority and differ only in the last mile of how you write."

## A 3-minute flow to rewrite an old opening

1. Rewrite the article title as a question (e.g., "How do you write an answer-first paragraph?").
2. Answer it in one sentence, packing in the single most important number or method name — this usually runs 30–50 words.
3. Add one more sentence with the most important qualifier (when it applies, why, or the effect) to reach 40–80 words.
4. Bold it, move it to the very top, and delete or push down the original warm-up paragraph.

Follow this flow and reworking the openings of 50 old articles on a site takes roughly 2–3 working days — the highest-ROI move in all of GEO optimization.

## Three common mistakes

- **Treating a summary as the answer.** "This article will introduce answer-first writing with examples" is a table of contents, not an answer. An answer must contain the substance itself.
- **Stuffing keywords instead of information.** An opening crammed with keywords but making no concrete claim gives the AI nothing to lift.
- **Making every paragraph fight to be first.** There's only one answer-first paragraph per article; the body's H2 sections each answer a *sub-question*, and the hierarchy has to stay clear. For the full layout, see [GEO Content Structure Template](/en/blog/geo-content-structure/).

## FAQ

**Q1: Is the 40–80 count including punctuation? How do I count mixed English and Chinese?**
Count by words for English and by characters for Chinese; don't fuss over punctuation. What matters is the feel of "read it in one glance, information complete." As a rule of thumb, one English word counts as one unit.

**Q2: Won't answer-first make readers leave right after the first paragraph?**
The ones who leave only ever wanted the answer — and you already lost them on the AI side; they were never staying. Readers who genuinely want depth actually trust you *more* because you led with the answer, and they read on into the argument.

**Q3: Does this apply to news or story-style content too?**
It applies fully to informational and how-to content; narrative content can compromise with "the intro is the point." The vast majority of SEO/GEO blog articles fall into the former group.

**Q4: Can the answer-first paragraphs across my articles copy each other?**
No. Each article maps to a different query, so the answer is necessarily different. A copy-pasted opening is the single easiest signal to get flagged as a templated, empty shell.
