# What Is E-E-A-T? And How to Actually Do It in 2026

**E-E-A-T stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness — the framework introduced in Google's Search Quality Rater Guidelines. It is not a score you can measure or a direct ranking factor; it's the standard Google trains its algorithms to approximate, its working definition of "what good content looks like."** The right way to "do E-E-A-T" is to leave verifiable trust evidence at both the page and site level.

## First, kill the biggest myth: E-E-A-T is not a ranking factor

Google's official position — across Search Central documentation and repeated public statements from spokespeople like Danny Sullivan and John Mueller — is consistent: **there is no score called E-E-A-T.** It lives in the guidelines given to human quality raters, and those raters' scores do not directly move any individual site's ranking. They're used to judge whether an algorithm update is heading in the right direction.

So "doing E-E-A-T" actually means one thing: **producing trust signals the algorithm can detect** — author information, entity consistency, first-hand evidence, external corroboration. Each of these signals influences ranking through a different mechanism.

## What are the four letters, and how do they differ?

| Letter | Meaning | Core question | On-page evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Experience | First-hand use or doing | "Have you actually done this yourself?" | Real photos, operation screenshots, records of failure, before/after data |
| Expertise | Domain knowledge | "Do you understand this topic?" | Content depth, correct terminology, author credentials |
| Authoritativeness | Recognized in the field | "Do others recognize you?" | External citations, brand searches, industry mentions |
| Trustworthiness | Accurate, honest, safe | "Is this website reliable?" | HTTPS, contact info, update dates, cited sources, willingness to name drawbacks |

Two things to note. The first E (Experience) was only added in December 2022, reflecting Google's response to the fact that AI can write in an expert-sounding voice but cannot fabricate genuine first-hand experience. And Google states plainly in the guidelines that **Trust is the center of the four** — the other three exist to support it.

## YMYL topics: the high bar for E-E-A-T

Money-and-health topics (Your Money or Your Life: medical, financial, legal, safety) are held to the strictest E-E-A-T standard. If your site touches these subjects, author credentials and a content-review process are not bonus points — they're the price of admission. General topics (SEO tutorials, travel, recipes) face a looser bar, and there "real experience" carries relatively more weight.

## The actionable E-E-A-T checklist for 2026

### Page level

1. **Attach a real author to every article**: name, headshot, one-line credentials, linked to an author page.
2. **Include first-hand evidence**: your own screenshots (not stock), tested data, project case studies, records of the mistakes you made. This is the differentiation that bulk AI content cannot replicate.
3. **Write clear dates**: include both `date_published` and `date_updated` — and when you update content, actually update it, don't just change the date.
4. **Cite your sources**: back your claims — link to official Google documentation, research, or original data.
5. **Be willing to state limits and drawbacks**: "this method doesn't apply in situation X" reads far more like an expert than wall-to-wall praise.

### Site level

1. **A complete About / Contact page**: who the entity is, where, and how to reach them.
2. **Author pages + Person schema**: structure your authors' credentials and link to their social profiles or publications (`sameAs`). Structuring this evidence with schema is what makes it machine-readable.
3. **Topical focus**: a site that goes deep on 1–3 topic clusters accumulates topical authority far more easily than one that writes about everything.
4. **Build brand search**: people searching directly for your brand name is one of the strongest authority signals the algorithm can measure.

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

**Q1: Is AI-written content inherently low on E-E-A-T?**
Google's official stance is that it "evaluates content quality regardless of how it's produced." The real weakness of AI content is Experience — it cannot write your personal data and case studies. AI drafting plus a human adding the first-hand material is the pragmatic 2026 workflow.

**Q2: How does a small site with an unknown author build Authoritativeness?**
Start with "authority within a scope": write one narrow topic deeper than anyone else on the web, supported by an interlocking topic cluster. Authority is relative to the topic, not a contest of site size. At the same time, cultivate channels that drive brand search — community, speaking, open-source tools.

**Q3: Can E-E-A-T be measured?**
There's no official score. Usable proxy metrics: brand-search volume trend (impressions of your own brand terms in the GSC query report), the number of external mentions, and the quality of search results for your author's name.

**Q4: I added an author box and schema but rankings didn't move — did I do it wrong?**
You didn't do it wrong, but that step only "makes the evidence machine-readable." The substance of E-E-A-T is the evidence itself — if there's no real experience or verifiable expertise inside the content, no amount of pretty markup has anything to convey.
