# Keyword Intent Types: Informational, Commercial, Transactional, Navigational

**Keyword intent splits into four types: informational (wants an answer), commercial (comparing and evaluating), transactional (ready to act), and navigational (headed to a specific site).** Classifying intent has exactly one purpose — matching each keyword to the correct page format. Get the intent-to-format pairing wrong and even great content will not rank.

## Why intent matters more than search volume

Because intent match is the very first gate Google ranks on. Someone searching "best seo tools" wants a comparison list; hand them a "what is SEO" explainer and it will not crack the top 10 no matter how well it is written — the top 10 are all list-style comparison posts, and that is the engine voting on intent. In 2026, AI engines run on the same logic: they pick sources whose *answer shape* fits the nature of the question. So keyword research always runs in this order: fix the intent first, then the page format, and only then write the content.

## The four intent types at a glance

| Type | User state | Word-level signals | Page format | Share (rule of thumb) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Informational (I) | Wants to know | what is, how to, tutorial, why | Blog post, guide | ~50–60% |
| Commercial (C) | Comparing | best, vs, review, compare, top | Comparison page, review, listicle | ~10–20% |
| Transactional (T) | Ready to act | price, buy, subscribe, deal, download | Product / service page | ~10% |
| Navigational (N) | Finding a place | brand name, brand + login | Homepage, official page | ~10–20% |

Shares drift by industry, but the general rule holds: **informational is the highest volume, commercial is the most valuable, transactional is closest to the sale, and navigational belongs only to the brand owner.**

## How to work each intent type

1. **Informational**: your workhorse content. The job is to cover the topic map and build topical authority, then use a micro-context close to steer authority toward your commercial pages. Do not jam a buy button into an informational post — wrong intent, and conversion trends toward zero.
2. **Commercial**: the type most worth investing in. Comparison pages, reviews, and "best X tools" lists all live here. Write them with genuine hands-on evidence, or they get flagged as low quality.
3. **Transactional**: the page's whole job is to erase the last doubts — transparent pricing, clear specs, an FAQ that handles objections. It is the fiercest SEO competition, usually supported by internal links poured in from commercial and informational pages.
4. **Navigational**: do not fight for searches on someone else's brand (the intent is to reach *their* official site — ranking there wins you no clicks); for your own brand's navigational terms, hold the number-one spot without exception.

## Three ways to read intent (most accurate to fastest)

- **Read the SERP (most accurate)**: drop the term into Google and look at what page formats fill the top 10. If 7 of the top 10 are list-style comparison posts, it is commercial — no guessing needed.
- **Read the modifiers (fastest)**: use the word-level signals in the table above to bulk-sort roughly, ideal when you are processing a few hundred terms at once.
- **Watch for mixed signals**: some terms carry mixed intent (for example "GEO tools" surfaces both informational and commercial results), which means the SERP has not settled yet — and that is actually an opening, because both page formats are worth a shot.

## Common misreads and how to fix them

- **Writing a commercial term as informational**: the most expensive mistake. "best geo tools" written as a GEO explainer → rewrite it as a listicle with a comparison table.
- **Forcing a mixed-intent term onto one side**: check the SERP's dominant format first, follow the majority, and win on content differentiation.
- **Ignoring language nuance**: some languages routinely drop the modifier — a query like "site migration" is usually a how-to (informational) even though the wording gives no hint — so classification leans even harder on reverse-engineering the SERP.

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

**Q1: Can one keyword have two intents at once?**
Yes — it is called mixed intent. The rule: follow whichever page format dominates the SERP; when it is a genuine 50/50, an informational article with a comparison table added is usually the safe play.

**Q2: Does intent change over time?**
It does. A new-technology term is mostly informational early on (everyone is still figuring it out), then commercial weight rises as it matures (everyone is choosing a tool). Re-reading the SERP for your priority terms every quarter is necessary maintenance.

**Q3: In the AI-engine era, does this classification still apply?**
It applies, and it matters more. AI cites guides when answering informational questions and cites reviews and comparisons when answering commercial ones — the answer shape has to fit before you even qualify to be cited.

**Q4: How does intent classification pair with topic clusters?**
The inside of a cluster *is* the intent division of labor: informational workhorse posts are the leaves, the commercial pillar page is the root, and the leaves pour authority into the root.
