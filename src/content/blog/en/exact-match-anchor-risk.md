# Why Buying Links All on One Keyword Is a Disaster

**The most fatal mistake when buying links isn't the act of buying itself — it's using the same exact-match keyword as the anchor text on every single one.** Natural citations are messy: brand names, bare URLs, and offhand phrases all show up. A hundred links marching in lockstep toward "some keyword" is you personally signing a note to Google that says: this batch was arranged by hand.

## The mechanism: uniformity is the evidence

A quick look at history makes it obvious. In 2012 Google launched the Penguin algorithm to crack down on link manipulation at scale, and the single most common pattern it caught was exact-match anchor text flooding. After Penguin 4.0 in 2016, that detection folded straight into the core algorithm and now runs in real time. More than a decade later, this is still the manipulation pattern machines catch most easily, because it stands out statistically:

- In a natural backlink profile, **brand names and bare URLs are usually the largest categories**, and exact-match keyword anchors are the rarest.
- If 90 of the 100 links you bought carry identical anchor text, that distribution doesn't appear on any normal website.
- Google doesn't need proof that you paid — the distribution itself is the proof.

Worse, the uniformity rarely stops at the anchor text. The same batch of links often shares other traits too: appearing in bulk within one narrow time window, coming from source sites that resemble one another, all pointing at the same target page. Stack those together and you have a complete manipulation profile.

## Why outsourced teams fall into this trap so easily

Because "the keyword the client specified = proof of delivery." A link vendor wants to show you the money went where it should, and the most intuitive way is to hang your target keyword on every link — which makes the KPI look great and gets the site flagged for manipulation. The disaster usually plays out in three acts:

1. **The order.** You hand over a list of target keywords, the vendor takes it at face value, and every link uses a term from that list.
2. **The honeymoon.** For the first one to three months rankings may genuinely move, you assume it's working, and you buy more.
3. **The reckoning.** The distribution grows more and more deformed, rankings start bleeding or fall off a cliff — and by now the links are scattered across dozens of sites you don't control.

## How to check whether you're caught

Use the Ahrefs Anchors report (Site Explorer → Anchors) to run a five-category audit: brand names, bare URLs, natural phrases, partial matches, and exact matches. Anchor text diversity is a whole discipline of its own — the point is to audit your own distribution across those five types. Focus on two things:

- **Exact-match share.** Google has never published a safe percentage, but if your exact-match anchor share is far above what naturally growing sites in your industry show (single digits for them, tens of percent for you), that's a red flag.
- **Anchor concentration.** Are your top three anchors all commercial keywords? On a natural site the top few are almost always brand-name and URL variants.

## The order to stop the bleeding once caught

1. **Stop buying immediately.** Don't let a single new exact-match anchor in — this takes priority over any remedy.
2. **Inventory the links you control.** List the ones you paid for and whose webmasters you can actually reach — these are the only part you can handle directly, and you can ask to have them changed to a brand-name anchor or taken down.
3. **Dilute with natural links.** Make new links you earn from here on (guest posts, digital PR, resource citations) lean heavily on brand names and bare URLs, letting the overall distribution drift back toward normal.
4. **Save disavow for last.** Only target sources that are yours, bought, and clearly junk — don't disavow every unfamiliar domain on sight.

Note: dilution is an engineering job measured in months, with no fast-forward button. The distribution is a statistical trait accumulated over the long run, and rushing to dump a pile of brand anchors in two weeks to "fix the ratio" is just another kind of unnatural.

## If you're going to buy anyway, hold these three lines

We don't recommend buying links, but the reality is that many sites already do. At the very least, hold these:

- **Leave the anchor text to the vendor**, or specify brand name / bare URL — never specify a commercial keyword.
- **Sources people actually read**: sites with real traffic and real content, not farms that exist to sell links.
- **Speed that looks like natural growth**: a brand-new site sprouting 200 links in its first month is an anomaly signal on its own.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

**Q1: Should there be zero exact-match anchors at all?**
No. Natural citations occasionally include an exact-match keyword anchor on their own (a directory-style resource list, for example). The question was never "any or none" — it's "is the share high enough to be statistically impossible to occur naturally."

**Q2: My rankings already dropped — will pulling all the bought links bring them back?**
No guarantee, and you may not need to pull them all. First confirm the timing relationship between the drop and this batch of links, then prioritize the most deformed part (same anchor, same time window, same source type). Algorithmic devaluation usually means the links get "neutralized" — after removal you rebuild on normal signals, which takes time.

**Q3: Can a competitor use this to hurt me (negative SEO)?**
In theory they can point exact-match junk links at you, but Google's default treatment of third-party spam links these years is to ignore them. What you should really worry about is always the batch you "paid someone to build" — because it's large, concentrated, and sustained.

**Q4: If buying links gets caught, do you get a notification?**
Only a Manual Action notifies you, in Google Search Console. Algorithmic devaluation comes with no notification at all — rankings just quietly stall or slide, which is exactly what makes it so dangerous.
