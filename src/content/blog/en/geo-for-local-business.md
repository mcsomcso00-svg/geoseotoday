# Local Business GEO: How to Get AI to Recommend You Nearby

**When someone asks AI "what's a good dentist near me" or "any coffee shops you'd recommend downtown," the AI doesn't pull from your blog. It pulls from three things: your Google Business Profile, third-party local reviews, and the machine-readable NAP (name, address, phone) on your website. The single biggest lever in local-business GEO is keeping one set of entity data identical across every platform — not publishing a few posts a week.** A small shop with steady reviews, a complete profile, and word-for-word consistent NAP everywhere gets recommended by AI far more often than a competitor who blogs diligently but left their profile blank.

## Why the GEO rules are different for a local business

General GEO is a content contest: whoever's article is clearly structured and full of quotable data gets into the AI's citation pool. Local business isn't that. The user asked for "near me," so before content even matters the AI has to solve two things first — who you are (entity disambiguation) and where you are (geographic association). The answers to those two questions don't live in your articles; they live in the business data scattered across the web under your name.

When ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overview answer local questions, they lean heavily on a maps-and-business data layer: Google's answers plug straight into Business Profile and map reviews, while ChatGPT and Perplexity pull public web pages, maps-type data, and review aggregators at the same time. The common thread: **they trust an entity that multiple independent sources corroborate over a single website's self-claim.** That's why roughly 80% of the work in local GEO happens off your website.

## The three sources AI pulls from locally — and what each needs

Distribute your effort to where AI actually reads, instead of betting everything on the blog:

| Source | What AI uses it to judge | What you do | Update cadence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Google Business Profile | Who you are, hours, category, location | Pick the most precise category, keep hours live, reply to reviews weekly, upload photos regularly | Weekly |
| Third-party local reviews | Reputation, trust, how others describe you | Actively request reviews, reply to negatives within 7 days, build past 4.0★ with volume | Ongoing |
| Structured NAP on site | Official fact anchor, disambiguation basis | Consistent NAP in the footer, add LocalBusiness schema, one page per location | Do it right once, update on change |
| Blog content | Subject-matter depth (secondary) | Local-scenario Q&A that links naturally to each location page | Only once the basics are done |

The key insight: for most local businesses, the marginal return on the first three rows dwarfs the fourth. If your profile category is wrong and your phone number is three different numbers on three platforms, no amount of articles will make an AI confident enough to recommend you.

## NAP consistency: the foundation of local GEO

NAP is Name, Address, Phone. AI (and Google) use it to decide that mentions scattered across the web all refer to the same business. The moment it's inconsistent, the AI's confidence in you drops — it can't be sure "the one on Main Street" and "the one on Main St." are the same place.

The three most common and most damaging inconsistencies:

1. **Messy phone formats**: the site says `(415) 123-4567`, Facebook says `415.123.4567`, and the profile has yet another version. AI does string comparison, and a formatting difference can get read as a different entity. Standardize one format everywhere.
2. **Inconsistent address forms**: "3rd Floor" vs "3F", suite included or not, abbreviated street names. Pick one official form and copy it verbatim across every platform.
3. **Marketing words jammed into the name**: the profile reads "XX Dental — Painless Implants · Sunday Hours" while the site just says "XX Dental." The name is the name; keyword-stuffing it violates Google's guidelines and blurs the entity for AI.

Self-check: compare your name, address, and phone across the site footer, Google Business Profile, Facebook, and industry directories (local listings, delivery platforms) one by one. Any character that differs is a hole to fix.

## A six-step checklist: from zero to "AI is willing to recommend you"

A local business doesn't need to write a pile of articles first. Do it in this order and lay the foundation before anything else:

1. **Claim and fully complete your Google Business Profile**: pick the most precise primary category ("Gelato shop" beats "Restaurant"), fill in hours, services, and 10+ real photos.
2. **Align NAP across the web**: using the three standards above, make the name/address/phone identical on your site, social profiles, and directories.
3. **Add LocalBusiness schema to the site**: on the homepage or each location page, add `LocalBusiness` structured data (or a more precise subtype like `Dentist` or `CafeOrCoffeeShop`) with name, address, telephone, openingHours, and geo coordinates. See [Schema for GEO](/en/blog/schema-for-geo/) for how.
4. **Start the review engine**: request reviews after checkout or service, and commit to replying to every review (especially negatives) within a week. The goal is "recent, plentiful, and responded-to."
5. **One page per location**: don't cram multiple branches onto one page. Give each location its own URL, its own NAP, and its own schema, so AI can associate it with the correct place.
6. **Only then write local content**: once the foundation is solid, write "local-scenario" Q&A (see the next section) to reinforce brand-entity signals. For the broader logic of brand consistency, see [Brand Search & Authority](/en/blog/brand-search-authority/).

## What to write for local content (after the foundation)

The blog isn't the main battlefield, but the right topics reinforce entity and expertise signals. Aim at the local questions users actually ask AI:

- Scenario questions: "which kind of clinic for a child's first dental visit," "how to pick a coffee shop for working on a laptop for hours."
- Local comparisons: "how service X differs between area A and area B."
- Common-knowledge answers: "5 things to ask a clinic before getting an implant."

Every piece uses an answer-first opening, includes concrete information, and links naturally back to the matching location page — forming a "content → location entity" path. Remember volume isn't the point: three solid, well-linked local pieces beat thirty generic ones.

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

**Q1: I don't have a storefront — I do on-site service. Can I still do local GEO?**
Yes. Google Business Profile supports a "service-area business" that hides the street address and sets a service region instead. The principles of NAP consistency and review-building are exactly the same; only the address field changes to a service area.

**Q2: Multiple locations — do I open a separate profile for each?**
Yes. Each physical location gets its own profile, its own location page on the site, and its own NAP and LocalBusiness schema. Sharing one page keeps AI from associating reviews and mentions with the correct place and actually dilutes each store's signal.

**Q3: If I get review-bombed or hit with fake negatives, does that kill my AI recommendations?**
A single negative has limited impact — AI looks at the overall pattern (rating, volume, response rate). Replying to negatives professionally and factually is itself a trust signal. Genuinely fake reviews can be handled through the platform's reporting flow, but don't stop accumulating real positives over a handful of negatives.

**Q4: How long until local GEO shows results?**
Completing the profile and aligning NAP usually shows up in Google's local results and maps within weeks. AI-engine recommendations depend on the next time each engine crawls and refreshes — typically weeks to a month or two. Review accumulation is the slowest but most durable piece.

**Q5: Can I just do the Google Business Profile and skip site schema?**
It may be enough within Google's ecosystem, but engines that crawl the open web — ChatGPT, Perplexity — need the structured NAP on your site as the official anchor. If you want cross-engine recommendations, site schema isn't optional.
