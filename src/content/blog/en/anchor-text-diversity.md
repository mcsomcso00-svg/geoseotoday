# Anchor Text Diversity in Practice: How to Mix the Five Types

**Anchor text diversity comes down to one idea: make your links look like other people are naturally citing you.** Genuine natural links are messy by default — brand names, bare URLs, natural phrases, partial matches, and exact keywords all mixed together. The opposite, an entire backlink profile pointing at the same exact keyword, is the textbook signal of manipulation.

## Why does anchor distribution matter so much?

Ever since the 2012 Penguin algorithm, Google has treated an "unnatural anchor text distribution" as one of the core fingerprints of link manipulation. After Penguin 4.0 in 2016 it runs in real time, baked directly into the core algorithm. In other words, this isn't a penalty that sweeps through once a year — it's a signal Google watches every single day.

Here's the key point: **Google has never published any "safe ratio."** The numbers you see floating around the web — "keep exact-match under 1%–2%" and the like — are all practitioner rules of thumb or reverse-engineered guesses, not official rules. Rather than memorizing a number, understand the principle: natural citations are messy, and manufactured manipulation is tidy.

## The five anchor types at a glance

| Type | Example (say you're "GeoSeoToday") | Role in natural links |
|---|---|---|
| Brand name | GeoSeoToday, GeoSeoToday | The biggest bucket — how people most often refer to you |
| Bare URL | semanticlab.example.com | Common in forums, comments, citation lists |
| Natural phrase / generic | "this article," "click here," "see this study" | Links dropped in the flow of writing |
| Partial match | "this anchor text guide," "their SEO tool" | A blend of keyword plus natural context |
| Exact match | "anchor text diversity" | The rarest — usually only in directory-style citations |

Look at a real site's backlink profile and you'll find brand name plus bare URL usually add up to more than half the total, while exact match is the smallest category of all. That is what "natural" looks like.

## In practice: audit yourself with the Ahrefs anchor report

1. Open Ahrefs → Site Explorer → enter your domain → pick the **Anchors** report on the left.
2. Export the report and sort each anchor into the five types above (a few hundred can be done by hand; for large volumes, hand it to AI to classify, then spot-check).
3. Calculate the share of each type, watching the **exact-match** column especially.
4. Compare against 2–3 naturally grown sites in your niche (the kind that aren't buying links) and see whether your exact-match share clearly stands out.

If your exact-match share is wildly high — say everyone else is in the single digits of a percent while you're above thirty — you don't need to wait for Google to notify you. That's the signal to act.

## What if exact-match is too high?

- **Stop immediately.** Don't acquire a single new link with an exact keyword anchor. This is the first and most important step.
- **Dilute with brand names and bare URLs.** Make every new link you acquire lead with brand names and bare URLs, letting the distribution drift back toward natural over time.
- **Don't go back and mass-edit old anchors.** Contacting webmasters to bulk-change anchor text is itself another unnatural pattern; unless the links are ones you control (like your own other sites), leave them in place and dilute with new links instead.
- **Check whether an agency caused it.** In many cases a link-building vendor used the client's target keyword on everything to "hit a KPI." Before taking over any legacy site, run an anchor report first.

## Principles for building new links

Don't walk in with a table that reads "brand 60%, bare URL 20%…" and try to hit exact quotas — that's still manipulation, just manipulation that looks a little better. The healthier approach:

- When you write guest posts or do digital PR, **let the other party decide how to link you** — you only make sure the brand name is spelled right.
- In places where you do control the anchor (author bios, directory listings), always use the brand name or a bare URL.
- Exact keyword anchors? Let them happen when they happen naturally, and never actively request them.

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

**Q1: Has Google ever published a safe anchor text ratio?**
No. Any claim that "exact-match must stay under X%" is a rule of thumb, not an official rule. There's only one principle: the distribution should look like natural citation, and natural citation is dominated by brand names and bare URLs.

**Q2: Do internal links need diverse anchor text too?**
Not by the same standard. Using descriptive keyword anchors on internal links is normal and helpful, and Google is far more tolerant of on-site anchors than external ones. That said, if a single page is pointed at by hundreds of identical on-site anchors, you can still afford to write them a little more naturally.

**Q3: My rankings dropped because of exact-match anchors — should I disavow?**
Confirm the cause first. If those links are ones you bought and are clearly low-quality, disavow is one option; if the share is just high but the sources are legitimate, diluting with new links is usually safer than disavowing.

**Q4: Should the brand name anchor always be written one fixed way?**
No. Full name, short form, mixed-language spellings are all fine — that variation is itself a sign of natural behavior. What matters is that it's spelled correctly and points to the right page.
