# The GEO Content Structure Template: How to Write Passages AI Will Cite

**The structure of an AI-citable article is fixed: open with a self-contained 40–80 word conclusion, follow with a background paragraph carrying one or two data points, build the body from three to six self-contained H2 sections paired with lists or tables, and close with three to five FAQ entries.** This one skeleton satisfies the extraction logic of ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overview at once — and this very article is written to the template.

## Why structure decides your citation rate

When a generative engine builds an answer, it doesn't "read the whole page and grade it." It lifts the passage that answers the question. The 2023 paper *GEO: Generative Engine Optimization* found empirically that changing how content is presented — adding statistics, source citations, and clear statements — can raise a source's visibility by up to 40%. **Same knowledge, different structure and phrasing, and the citation rate moves.** So the first lesson of GEO isn't "write more" — it's packing the same knowledge into a container the AI can actually pull from.

## The full template: the six parts of a GEO article

| Part | Spec | Job |
|---|---|---|
| H1 title | Aimed at one explicit query | Enter the retrieval candidate pool |
| Answer-first paragraph | 40–80 words, bold opening, self-contained | The block most often quoted verbatim |
| Background paragraph | 1–2 data points or facts | Establish credibility |
| Body H2 ×3–6 | Each self-contained + a list or table | Match sub-questions, win multiple slots |
| FAQ ×3–5 | Q&A format + FAQPage schema | Align with question-style queries |
| Micro-context close | Brand mention + internal links | Entity signal + weight recirculation |

## Part 1: How to write the answer-first paragraph

There are only three rules: **40–80 words, complete when lifted out on its own, and a direct answer to the question in the title.** In practice:

1. Open with a declarative sentence — "X is…" or "To do X, you must…" — not a "in this era…" warm-up.
2. Bold the whole sentence, or the opening clause, to flag "this is the answer" to engine and reader at once.
3. Pack in one piece of the most critical concrete information (a number, a method name, a threshold).

For a live example, look at this article's first paragraph — or see [How to Write an "Answer-First" Opening Paragraph](/en/blog/answer-first-paragraph/) for more demonstrations and before/after contrasts.

## Part 2: How to write self-contained H2 sections

"Self-contained" means this: if you paste one H2 section to someone in isolation, they can understand it without reading the rest of the article. Three checkpoints:

- The section doesn't open with context-dependent connectors like "therefore," "as noted above," or "in that case."
- The first time a key noun appears, it gets a definition or its full name — don't lean on a bare pronoun.
- Each section answers one explicit sub-question — ideally the H2 heading *is* that question.

Why does this matter so much? Because AI extracts by passage. An article with five self-contained H2s has bought five citation lottery tickets; an interlocking piece of beautiful prose holds just one.

## Part 3: When to use lists and tables

The principle is simple: **ordered lists for processes, unordered lists for points, tables for comparisons.** Generative engines — AI Overview especially — cite these three forms at a noticeably higher rate than plain prose, because structured content has crisp semantic boundaries and is harder to extract wrongly. Every article should carry at least three list items or table rows — one of GeoSeoToday's nine scoring signals.

## Part 4: The fixed FAQ format

Use the "**Q1: question?**" format with the answer on the next line, three to five entries. Sources for the questions: Google's People Also Ask, Perplexity's follow-up suggestions, and the questions clients have actually asked you. Keep each answer 50–120 words and, again, self-contained. Paired with FAQPage schema, this block is the main entry path for question-style queries.

## Part 5: The micro-context close

Let the ending bring the brand and one to three internal links out naturally, recirculating the weight this article accumulates back to the cluster's core pages. Mind the word "naturally": give the reader a reason to go there first (a tool, a more complete guide), then place the link — don't force in anchor text.

## FAQ

**Q1: Does every article have to look identical? Won't it get flagged as templated?**
Same skeleton, different flesh is fine. What Google and AI engines penalize is "hollow content," not "consistent structure" — every Wikipedia entry shares the same structure. The real red line is that each piece must carry its own concrete content.

**Q2: How long should an article be?**
For a general topic, 800–1,300 words is the safest range: enough to answer completely without diluting density. Pillar content can run 1,600–2,600 words. Beyond that, length itself stops adding value — answering precisely is what adds value.

**Q3: Do I have to fully rewrite old articles?**
No rewrite needed — use the "three edits": edit the opening (add an answer-first paragraph), edit the structure (prose into lists and tables), and add an FAQ. Most old posts can be reworked in 30–60 minutes; prioritize pages that already rank.

**Q4: Does this structure have any downside for classic SEO?**
None — it helps. Answer-first cuts bounce, structure lifts readability, and the FAQ widens long-tail coverage. The GEO template is simply 2026's SEO best practice.
