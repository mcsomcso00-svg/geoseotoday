# Broken Link Building: Turn a Competitor's Dead Link Into Your Backlink

**Broken link building means finding a page — usually a competitor's or another site in your niche — that links out to a resource which no longer exists, building a more complete replacement, and telling the linking site "this link is dead, here's a working alternative." Typical success rates run 10–18%, slightly better than cold guest-post pitches, because you're fixing a real problem for the site owner instead of just asking for a favor.**

## Why dead links are an underrated link opportunity

Any site that's been live for more than two or three years accumulates a certain share of dead links — a tool they cited got shut down, a government page got restructured, a research paper they referenced moved URLs. Site owners rarely audit their own old posts, so these dead links often sit untouched for years; readers who hit a 404 rarely bother writing in about it. That's exactly the opportunity: you're not asking the owner to "do you a favor and add a link" — you're telling them their page is broken and you can fix it. That shift in framing is why broken link building clears a lower psychological bar than a typical outreach pitch — most site owners are more willing to fix a dead link than to add a brand-new one.

## Step 1: Find candidate pages worth mining

Not every dead link is worth chasing. Focus on pages in your niche with real link equity:

1. **Target resource lists and long-form tutorials** — these tend to cite the most external tools and data, so dead-link density (and your hit rate) is highest here.
2. **Pull dead-link lists with Ahrefs or Screaming Frog** — run an outbound link check on a candidate domain and filter for 404 or 410 response codes.
3. **Cross-check the link equity of the linking page** — prioritize pages with high DR (Domain Rating) where the dead link's surrounding paragraph closely matches content you can actually provide. Pages under DR 15 usually aren't worth the time.
4. **Note what the original dead link pointed to** — this determines exactly what your replacement content needs to cover.

After one filtering pass, 100 candidate dead links typically narrow down to 15–25 targets genuinely worth pursuing; the rest either sit on low-equity pages or don't match any content you can realistically produce.

## Step 2: Build a replacement that's better than the original

The most common way broken link building fails isn't a shortage of dead links — it's a rushed replacement. The site owner will ask themselves, "why should I point this link at you instead," and you need a concrete answer. Match your replacement to the type of resource that died:

| Original dead-link type | What the replacement needs |
|---|---|
| Statistics page / research report | Data updated to the current year, with the original source cited |
| Free tool / calculator | Equal or greater functionality, actually usable, no signup wall |
| Tutorial article | Covers every subtopic of the original, plus steps or examples it lacked |
| Downloadable resource / template | Compatible format, plus an editable version the original didn't offer |

Making the replacement good enough that the site owner thinks "this is clearly better than what I had" is the single biggest driver of reply rate — no amount of outreach polish rescues weak content.

## Step 3: The outreach email needs exactly one message

The email doesn't need to introduce your site or company. It needs three things: where the dead link sits, what it was pointing to and why it's broken, and what your replacement is. Template: "Noticed the link to [original resource name] in your [page title] under the '[paragraph keyword]' section is returning a 404. We put together an updated [replacement resource] covering the same topic with current data — happy to have you swap it in if it's useful to your readers." Specific paragraph location, specific proof of breakage, specific replacement — the owner doesn't need to re-verify anything before deciding whether it's worth their time.

## Step 4: Outreach and follow-up cadence

1. **Build the list** — organize your 15–25 filtered targets into a spreadsheet: domain, URL of the dead link, contact email.
2. **First email** — use the template above, include your replacement link, skip the long self-introduction.
3. **Follow up after 10 days** — a short reminder to anyone who hasn't replied, just confirming the email arrived.
4. **Track conversion** — across 180 broken-link outreach emails tracked by GeoSeoToday for two client accounts, the average reply rate was 29% and the actual link-swap success rate was 14%, sitting between resource-page outreach (19%) and guest-post outreach (6%).

At 20 targets per round, a 14% success rate typically nets 2–3 editorial backlinks per round. Candidate discovery takes longer than resource-page outreach, but because the linking pages already carry some link equity by definition, the backlinks you land tend to come from higher-authority tutorial or resource pages.

## Honest limitations

Broken link building isn't a channel you can mindlessly scale. Three limits worth knowing up front: candidate discovery is time-consuming — one round of 15–25 targets usually takes 3–4 hours of data gathering and filtering; replacement content can be expensive to produce, since matching a full research report means building something of comparable quality, not a quick summary post; and there's no guarantee the owner swaps the link — even a strong replacement gets ignored by owners who've abandoned the page entirely. Treat it as one channel in a broader link-building mix, not a standalone solution.

## FAQ

**Q1: Which converts better, broken link building or resource-page outreach?**
Based on GeoSeoToday's tracked numbers, resource-page outreach (19%) edges out broken link building (14%), but both beat cold guest-post pitches (6%) by a wide margin. Resource-page outreach has a lower barrier to entry, so run that first; broken link building pairs well with evergreen content you already plan to refresh, since replacement production cost is higher.

**Q2: How do I check for dead links without paid tools?**
You can check pages manually with a browser extension, or narrow the field with a Google search operator like `site:competitordomain.com "further reading"` and click through manually — but this is far slower than a batch crawler like Ahrefs or Screaming Frog. At minimum, run one pass with a tool's free tier.

**Q3: The site already swapped the link — do I need to keep following up?**
No need to actively check in, but it's worth re-auditing your earned links quarterly — some site owners wipe out old links wholesale during a redesign, and a short reminder email can win the link back.

**Q4: Does this work for a brand-new site?**
Yes, but conversion rates run lower since new sites lack an existing trust baseline. Lean on the quality of the replacement itself to make the case, and back the email with specific, current data — don't rely on brand recognition you don't have yet.
