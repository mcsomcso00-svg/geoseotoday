# Resource Page Link Building: Find Curated Lists and Get Added

**Resource pages are curated lists that site owners maintain specifically to collect links, so outreach success rates typically run 15–25% — far higher than the 5–10% you get from cold guest-post pitches. Use 6 advanced search operators to find relevant resource pages, then pitch inclusion with a single sentence explaining why you belong, backed by a 4-step outreach workflow.**

## Why resource pages convert better than cold outreach

Most link outreach fails because you're asking the site owner for a favor — their site gets nothing in return for adding your link. Resource pages are different: the page exists specifically to collect links. Maintaining a curated list is itself proof to readers that "we've vetted this for you," and every quality link added strengthens that value proposition. You're not asking for a handout — you're offering material that makes their page more complete. That difference in motivation shows up directly in reply rates: across 240 resource-page outreach emails tracked by GeoSeoToday for three client accounts, the average reply rate was 38% and the positive-inclusion rate was 19% — more than triple the same period's guest-post outreach (12% reply, 6% success).

## Step 1: Surface resource pages with advanced search operators

Resource pages rarely brand themselves with the phrase "resource page," so a plain keyword search in Google turns up little. What actually works is combining your topic with the title patterns resource pages commonly use:

| Operator | What it finds | Example (topic: SEO tools) |
|---|---|---|
| `intitle:resources SEO tools` | Pages with "resources" in the title | intitle:resources SEO tools |
| `intitle:"useful links" SEO` | Pages titled "useful links" | intitle:"useful links" SEO tools |
| `"helpful resources" SEO tools` | Body text containing this phrase | "helpful resources" SEO tools |
| `inurl:resources SEO` | URL path containing /resources/ | inurl:resources SEO tools |
| `"link roundup" SEO tools` | Recurring curated-link roundups | "link roundup" SEO tools 2026 |
| `site:.edu "recommended resources" SEO` | University recommendation lists, high authority | site:.edu "recommended resources" SEO |

You don't need to run all six at once. The first three typically surface 20–40 candidate pages. The `.edu` operator returns fewer hits but each link carries more weight — run it as a second pass once your initial candidate list is exhausted.

## Step 2: Filter candidates against three qualifying conditions

Not every page that surfaces is worth outreach. Filter with three conditions:

1. **Recently maintained**: Check the last-modified date or the year of the newest entry. Anything untouched for 18+ months is likely abandoned — outreach there goes nowhere.
2. **Genuinely on-topic**: Are the other links in the same niche, or is it a grab-bag list of unrelated sites? The latter may already be flagged as a link farm, and joining it can drag down your own link profile.
3. **Real traffic behind it**: Check the domain in Ahrefs or a similar tool for organic search traffic. An empty-shell site with zero traffic isn't worth the inclusion.

Running 20–40 candidates through these three filters typically leaves 8–15 targets genuinely worth your time.

## Step 3: One sentence explaining why you belong

The biggest mistake in resource-page outreach is leading with a full introduction of your company. Site owners don't have time to read a self-promotional essay — they want to know one thing: **what does adding this link do for my readers**. So the pitch needs exactly one sentence, in the format "Our [resource] helps your readers [specific benefit], which fits the gap in your [specific section of the list]."

Template: "We built a [free GEO readiness checker] that lets your readers [measure in 30 seconds whether their content has a shot at being cited by AI] — it would fit well under the 'SEO Tools' section of your list, which currently leans toward traditional ranking tools." Naming the tool, the specific use case, and the specific gap in their list lets the site owner judge relevance at a glance.

## Step 4: Outreach and tracking cadence

1. **Build the list**: Organize your 8–15 filtered targets into a tracking sheet with domain, contact email, and the relevant section of their page.
2. **First email**: Use the one-sentence pitch from Step 3, link your resource, skip the long bio.
3. **Follow up after 7 days**: For non-responders, send one short follow-up confirming the email arrived — don't repeat the full pitch.
4. **Log outcomes**: Track every result, added or not. Rejected or unresponsive domains go on a retry list for a future round with different material — never re-pitch the same resource to the same owner.

At a 19% inclusion rate across a 15-target round, you can expect 2–3 real editorial links per round. Run two rounds a month and that's 50–70 resource-page links a year, most of them static pages unlikely to be removed — a stronger compounding return than a one-off guest post.

## FAQ

**Q1: Do resource page links carry less weight than other backlinks?**
Per-link value depends on the domain's own authority and traffic, not the page type. The advantage of resource pages isn't higher per-link value — it's a higher success rate at lower acquisition cost, making them a solid base layer in a broader link portfolio.

**Q2: Is it still worth pitching a list that already has many similar tools?**
Yes, but the pitch needs to name a specific differentiator — free tier, supported languages, a particular feature — rather than a generic "we're a good tool too" claim.

**Q3: How often should I re-run the resource page search?**
Quarterly. New resource pages appear continuously, and your existing candidate list needs pruning for pages that have stopped updating or been redesigned.

**Q4: Should I ever pay for resource page inclusion?**
No. A genuinely healthy resource page doesn't accept paid placement — a list that demands payment for a link is itself a signal of a link farm, and joining does more harm than good.
