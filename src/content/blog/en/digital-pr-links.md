# How Digital PR and Press Releases Earn Natural Links

**Digital PR is really about building the material a reporter *needs*, so that coverage and links follow as a natural result.** It's one of the few white-hat methods that earns high-authority editorial links at scale — you can't buy a link on a major news site, but one good story can win you coverage from ten outlets at once. Blasting a boilerplate press release across a distribution wire, by contrast, mostly gets you syndicated pages nobody reads, with link value close to zero.

## How is digital PR different from sending a press release?

Plenty of people conflate the two, then spend a wire-service budget while expecting digital-PR results. Pulled apart:

| Comparison | Press release wire | Digital PR |
|---|---|---|
| Logic | Pay to place the release on N sites | Make a reporter *want* to write about you |
| Link type | Syndicated pages, often nofollow/sponsored | Editorial in-body links given by choice |
| Content | What the company wants to say | What the reporter's readers want to read |
| SEO value | Close to zero (Google says it ignores them) | High, and comes with brand-search growth |

Google stated its position on press-release links a long time ago: links inside distribution services should carry nofollow, and mass-blasting releases to build links counts as a link scheme. So a wire service is fine to use — as a notification channel for reaching media — but don't treat "published on 50 sites" as a backlink result.

## What do journalists want? Four actionable story angles

Digital PR succeeds or fails on the material, not the list. What reporters lack is "credible, fresh facts that tie into what's happening now." These four angles are ones almost any site can produce:

1. **Original data studies**: use your own data or a survey to answer a question the media writes about often. For example, an e-commerce platform analyzes tens of thousands of its own orders and publishes a "Consumer Returns Behavior Report." The data must be genuine and the method transparent — **fabricating statistics is a shortcut to destroying your credibility**; if a reporter fact-checks and finds it faked, your brand lands on a blacklist.
2. **Rapid response to the news (newsjacking)**: when an algorithm update, industry policy, or trending event hits, be first to offer expert interpretation. The window is measured in hours, so this suits teams that know their field cold.
3. **Free tools or interactive content**: a calculator, a checker, a map-based visualization. If the tool solves a problem the reporter's readers care about, coverage has a reason to link to it.
4. **Counterintuitive expert takes**: an evidence-backed challenge to the industry consensus. The risk is that your case genuinely has to hold up; the reward is a differentiated, memorable hook.

## Execution: from material to link

1. **Validate the angle first**: search whether the media has covered this topic in the past year and whose data they cited. Someone has written about it but the data is stale = a golden opportunity.
2. **Build an asset page**: turn the research into a page on your site (not a PDF), with the key numbers up front, downloadable charts, and a ready-made citation format. This page is the landing spot for the backlinks.
3. **Build the media list**: find the reporters who have written on the same topic (not a general newsroom inbox), 1–2 per outlet. List quality matters far more than quantity.
4. **Pitch one to one**: in under 150 words, spell out "what question your data answers and the single most striking number." Include a link to the full data — don't use an attachment.
5. **Follow up and repurpose**: after coverage runs, the same material can be pitched again to regional outlets, industry newsletters, and podcasts — one fish, many meals.

## Setting realistic expectations

Digital PR is a batting-average game: 50 pitches that turn into 3–5 pieces of coverage is a solid result, and one piece in a mainstream outlet often gets re-cited by other sites, bringing a long tail of follow-on links. Its cost structure is "front-loaded, long-compounding" — the exact opposite of buying links, which is "instant delivery, can zero out at any time." A small site on a tight budget can start with newsjacking and local outlets to build the muscle, without taking on national media from day one.

## Frequently asked questions (FAQ)

**Q1: A small company has no data and no news — how can it do digital PR?**
A survey is your data source: a well-designed poll of 300–500 people in your target group costs far less than you'd think. The "local angle" is also the small player's weapon — national data isn't yours to break, but "an industry observation for one county or city" is exactly what local media are starved for.

**Q2: Should I still use a press release distribution service (a wire)?**
You can, but redefine the goal: it's a notification channel to make media *aware* of something, not a backlink source. The real links come from reporters' follow-on coverage. Book the wire fee as a PR cost, not against your backlink budget.

**Q3: An outlet covered me but gave no link — what do I do?**
First, send a polite thank-you email asking whether they can add a source link; the success rate is higher than you'd expect, because crediting a source is a plus for the reporter. Even if they don't, an unlinked brand mention is itself an entity signal that helps your brand-search volume.

**Q4: Do I need to control the anchor text on links earned through digital PR?**
No, and you shouldn't. The reporter decides how to write it, and the result is naturally a mix of brand names, bare URLs, and natural phrases — which is exactly the healthiest anchor-text distribution. See [Anchor Text Diversity in Practice](/en/blog/anchor-text-diversity/) for the details.
